I was convinced I had a shot, until the rejections started pouring in. **FAANGS didnâ€™t want me.** My CV was getting filtered out by ATS systems, my LeetCode skills were probably not good enough, and I was starting to question if I would ever land anything decent.

## **1. Building a Profile That Stands Out (The CV has to be more than a simple piece of paper!)**

I knew my CV had to be **sharp**. Not just a list of experiences, but something that would make recruiters pause and think, *"This guyâ€™s worth a call."* I focused on:
- **CV formulation:** I found many sources on the internet that can help people build strong ATS-oriented CVs. In particular I found this guy [Sajaad Khader](https://www.youtube.com/@SajjaadKhader) sharing super interesting advice to get a job/internship in a big tech company. The basics is to display the impact you had in every single bulletpoint you write on your CV, they have to contain numbers.
- **Personal projects:** I had to build projects, or to take part in events that could make me gain experience. It was about having a nice GitHub, it was about having something interesting to talk about in interviews. I went back on my first year C project. The Sokoban game to program in C, it was tough, at this time I was not good at all in low-level programming languages. So I focused on learning or reviewing the basics of computer science in general (basically data structures, memory management, a project structure, etc). I tried to reproduce the project and this time to finish it. It was worth the time and I was sincerely very happy when I extended the project with a (very simple) UI interface with Streamlit to play the game. I also took part in a Hackathon in June 2025. It was organized by the French tech ministery in order to improve open-source solutions replacing big tech solutions to improve the European sovereignty. 

### **Interview Prep: The LeetCode Grind**
I will be honest: doing my first DSA exercice I hated LeetCode. But I did it anyway.
I started by reviewing Data Structures and Algorithms from a first year computer science course. It was not easy. Arrays, sets, hashmaps, linked-lists, etc. But understanding these data structures is key and really made me understand the advantages/downsides of these different elements. I used [AlgoMap](https://www.algomap.io/) to track my progress and make sure I wasnâ€™t missing any key topics. Instead of trying as many exercices as I could, I focused on learning key patterns that would allow me to be able to solve any LeetCode exercice. I also did **mock interviews** with friends, it helped me stop blanking out when someone asked me to explain my thought process, solving an easy exercice may be easy but explaining your thoughts in real-time really is difficult and you have to practice to become better doing it.
I also practiced **behavioral questions**. Yes, theyâ€™re annoying, but they matter a lot. Especially before Amazon interviews, I focused on their Leadership Principles (LP) and prepared a good number of stories that I could adapt to any LP that was asked.
